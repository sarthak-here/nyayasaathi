CONSUMER PROTECTION ACT 2019 — KEY PROVISIONS
==============================================

OVERVIEW
The Consumer Protection Act 2019 replaced the older 1986 Act and significantly strengthened consumer rights in India. It introduced new provisions for e-commerce, product liability, unfair trade practices, and a Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA).

WHO IS A CONSUMER?
Under Section 2(7), a consumer is a person who:
- Buys any goods for consideration (paid or promised)
- Hires or avails of any service for consideration
- Uses goods bought by another person with approval
- Uses services availed by another person

A person is NOT a consumer if:
- Goods/services are bought for commercial resale purposes
- Services are availed for commercial purpose with large scale commercial activities

CONSUMER RIGHTS (Section 2(9))
1. Right to Safety — protection against hazardous goods and services
2. Right to Information — about quality, quantity, potency, price of goods/services
3. Right to Choice — access to a variety of goods/services at competitive prices
4. Right to be Heard — interests to be considered in appropriate forums
5. Right to Seek Redressal — against unfair trade practices and exploitation
6. Right to Consumer Education — awareness about rights and remedies

WHAT ARE UNFAIR TRADE PRACTICES?
Section 2(47) defines unfair trade practices including:
- False representation of quality, standard, grade or composition
- False claim that goods are new when reconditioned or old
- False representation about sponsorship, approval, or affiliation
- Making false claim about price of goods/services
- Giving false warranties about performance, efficacy, length of life
- Not issuing bills/receipts for goods/services sold
- Refusing to take back defective goods within 30 days

DEFICIENCY IN SERVICE
Section 2(11) — deficiency means:
- Fault, imperfection, shortcoming in quality, nature, manner of performance
- Inadequacy in service required by law or contract
Examples: delayed delivery, poor quality service, not honoring warranty, overcharging

WHERE TO FILE A COMPLAINT?
Complaints are filed based on the value of goods/services and compensation claimed:

DISTRICT CONSUMER DISPUTES REDRESSAL COMMISSION (DCDRC):
- Claims up to Rs. 1 crore
- Located in every district headquarters
- File complaint in district where seller resides, works, or has branch, OR where cause of action arose

STATE CONSUMER DISPUTES REDRESSAL COMMISSION (SCDRC):
- Claims above Rs. 1 crore up to Rs. 10 crore
- Also hears appeals from DCDRC
- Located at state capital

NATIONAL CONSUMER DISPUTES REDRESSAL COMMISSION (NCDRC):
- Claims above Rs. 10 crore
- Also hears appeals from SCDRC
- Located in New Delhi

EDAAKHIL — ONLINE COMPLAINT PORTAL
Website: edaakhil.nic.in
Consumers can file complaints online, upload documents, pay fees, and track cases.

HOW TO FILE A CONSUMER COMPLAINT?
Step 1: Send a legal notice to the seller/service provider giving 15 days to resolve
Step 2: If unresolved, prepare complaint on plain paper or use format

COMPLAINT FORMAT:
To,
The President,
District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission,
[District Name]

COMPLAINT UNDER CONSUMER PROTECTION ACT, 2019

Complainant: [Full Name, Address, Phone, Email]
Opposite Party: [Name of company/seller, address]

FACTS:
1. [Date and details of purchase/transaction]
2. [What went wrong — defect, deficiency, fraud]
3. [Steps taken to resolve with seller and response received]

RELIEF SOUGHT:
1. Replacement/refund of Rs. [amount]
2. Compensation for mental agony and harassment of Rs. [amount]
3. Litigation costs of Rs. [amount]

DOCUMENTS ATTACHED:
- Invoice/receipt
- Warranty card
- Legal notice sent and acknowledgment
- Correspondence with seller
- Photos/evidence of defect

I solemnly declare that the contents are true to my knowledge.

[Signature]
[Name]
[Date]

FILING FEES (District Commission)
- Claims up to Rs. 5 lakh: No fee
- Rs. 5 lakh to Rs. 10 lakh: Rs. 200
- Rs. 10 lakh to Rs. 20 lakh: Rs. 400
- Rs. 20 lakh to Rs. 50 lakh: Rs. 1,000
- Rs. 50 lakh to Rs. 1 crore: Rs. 2,000

TIME LIMIT TO FILE COMPLAINT
2 years from the date on which cause of action arose. Courts can condone delay if sufficient cause shown.

PRODUCT LIABILITY (Chapter VI)
New provision in 2019 Act. A product manufacturer/seller is liable for harm caused by a defective product.
Liability exists when:
- Product has a manufacturing defect
- Product deviates from manufacturing specifications
- Product does not conform to express warranty
- Product fails to contain adequate instructions for correct use

CENTRAL CONSUMER PROTECTION AUTHORITY (CCPA)
New body under 2019 Act empowered to:
- Investigate violations of consumer rights
- Recall defective goods, order refunds
- File class action complaints on behalf of consumers
- Impose penalties on misleading advertisements (up to Rs. 10 lakh for first offence)

E-COMMERCE COMPLAINTS
The Act specifically covers e-commerce transactions. Online marketplaces must:
- Display authentic details of sellers
- Ensure no fake/counterfeit goods are listed
- Have clear return, refund, and exchange policies
- Not manipulate prices

COMMON CONSUMER COMPLAINTS
1. Defective electronic products — mobile phones, appliances
2. E-commerce — wrong product delivered, refund denied, damaged goods
3. Insurance — claim rejected, policy mis-sold
4. Real estate — delayed possession, quality issues
5. Healthcare — medical negligence, overcharging
6. Banking — unauthorized debit, hidden charges
7. Telecom — service disruption, wrongful billing
8. Education — fee refund, false promises about placement

==========================================================================
IF YOU ARE THE OPPOSITE PARTY (SELLER/SERVICE PROVIDER) — DEFENSE RIGHTS
==========================================================================

YOUR RIGHTS AS OPPOSITE PARTY IN CONSUMER COMPLAINT:
- Right to file a written reply/version within 30 days of receiving notice from the Commission.
- Right to cross-examine the complainant.
- Right to produce evidence, expert witnesses, and technical reports.
- Right to appeal: District Commission → State Commission → National Commission → Supreme Court.

WHAT COMPLAINANT MUST PROVE:
- That they are a "consumer" (not a commercial reseller).
- That there was a deficiency in service or defect in goods as defined by the Act.
- That the deficiency/defect was caused by your negligence or fault — not by misuse, normal wear, or third-party factors.
- The quantum of loss suffered.

COMMON DEFENSES FOR BUSINESSES:
Product misuse: If the consumer misused the product contrary to instructions, the defect was caused by them — not a manufacturing defect.
Normal wear and tear: Products degrade over time. Defects appearing after the warranty period due to use are not your liability.
Terms and conditions: If the consumer breached contract terms (e.g., returned after the return window), your refusal is justified — show the agreed terms.
Third-party fault: If the defect was caused by a third party (courier damage, power surge) and not by you, demonstrate this with evidence.
Complainant is not a consumer: If goods were bought for commercial resale or large-scale commercial activity, the Consumer Protection Act does not apply.
Service was rendered correctly: Produce service records, inspection reports, photographs, and expert opinions showing the service met contractual and regulatory standards.

LIMITATION / JURISDICTION:
Complaint must be filed within 2 years — if filed beyond this, apply for dismissal on limitation.
Jurisdiction is where you have your place of business or where the cause of action arose — challenge if complaint filed in wrong forum.

APPEAL ROUTE:
District Commission order → Appeal to State Commission within 45 days (deposit 50% of awarded amount or Rs. 25,000, whichever is less).
State Commission order → National Commission within 30 days.
NCDRC order → Supreme Court within 30 days.

SETTLEMENT:
Consumer cases can be settled at any stage. Mediation is now mandatory before the Commission proceeds to hearing. Settling early avoids reputational damage and litigation costs.
