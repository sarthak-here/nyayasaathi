NEGOTIABLE INSTRUMENTS ACT 1881 — CHEQUE BOUNCE (Section 138–142)

Cheque dishonour (bounce) is both a civil wrong and a criminal offence in India. Section 138 NI Act makes it a criminal offence punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years or fine up to twice the cheque amount, or both.

WHEN IS CHEQUE BOUNCE AN OFFENCE?
A cheque is "dishonoured" (bounced) when:
1. The account has insufficient funds
2. The amount exceeds the arrangement
3. The account is closed or frozen
4. Payment stopped by drawer

NOTE: Cheque bounce is an offence ONLY if the cheque was given for discharge of a legally enforceable debt or liability. Post-dated cheques given as security may not attract Section 138 in all cases.

PROCEDURE TO FILE A CASE — STEP BY STEP

Step 1: Receive Memo/Return from Bank
When the bank returns (dishonours) the cheque, get the return memo specifying the reason for dishonour. Keep this carefully — it is crucial evidence.

Step 2: Send Legal Notice within 30 Days
Within 30 days of receiving the dishonour memo, send a WRITTEN LEGAL NOTICE to the drawer (person who gave the cheque) by registered post/speed post demanding payment.
The notice must:
- State the cheque details (number, date, amount, bank)
- State that the cheque was dishonoured
- Demand payment within 15 days of receipt of notice

Step 3: Wait 15 Days
Give the drawer 15 days from receipt of the notice to make payment.

Step 4: File Complaint within 30 Days of Expiry of Notice Period
If no payment is made within 15 days, file a criminal complaint under Section 138 NI Act before the Judicial Magistrate (First Class) within 30 days of expiry of the notice period.
The complaint must be filed where:
- The cheque was presented for payment (bank branch where you deposited it), OR
- Where the cheque was drawn (drawer's bank), OR
- Where the payee conducts business

JURISDICTION (after 2015 Amendment): Case is filed where the bank branch of the PAYEE (the person who received the cheque) is located.

EVIDENCE REQUIRED
1. Original dishonoured cheque
2. Bank return memo / dishonour memo
3. Legal notice sent (copy + registered post receipt)
4. Acknowledgment of receipt of notice by accused (or track report showing delivery/refusal)
5. Account statement showing the cheque amount owed

PUNISHMENT IF CONVICTED
- Imprisonment up to 2 years, OR
- Fine up to twice the cheque amount, OR
- Both imprisonment and fine
Court typically orders payment of the cheque amount to the complainant.

LIMITATION PERIOD
The complaint must be filed within 30 days of expiry of 15-day notice period. Missing this deadline is fatal — the case becomes time-barred.

INTERIM COMPENSATION (Section 143A NI Act)
Court can order accused to pay up to 20% of the cheque amount as interim compensation during trial. If accused acquitted, complainant must refund it.

APPEAL (Section 148): Appellate court must order deposit of minimum 20% of fine awarded before hearing appeal. This prevents frivolous appeals.

COMPOUNDING OF OFFENCE
Cheque bounce cases can be settled (compounded) between parties at any stage with court permission. Drawer pays full cheque amount + interest + costs. Case gets quashed.
Lok Adalat: Fastest way to settle. Go to Lok Adalat through district court — settlement is final, no appeal, no court fees.

CIVIL REMEDY IN ADDITION
You can ALSO file a civil suit for recovery of money (Section 6 of Limitation Act — 3 year limitation period) in addition to the criminal complaint. These run simultaneously.

MULTIPLE CHEQUES
If multiple cheques from same drawer bounce, file separate complaints for each cheque — each is a separate offence.

DEFENCES AVAILABLE TO ACCUSED
1. Cheque was given as security, not for debt repayment
2. Debt was not legally enforceable
3. Notice was not properly served
4. Complaint filed after limitation period
5. Amount on cheque was altered
6. Stop payment was for valid reason (fraud/coercion by payee)

IF YOU ARE THE ACCUSED (drawer)
1. Check if notice was properly served (address, registered post)
2. Check if complaint was filed within limitation period
3. Offer to pay and seek compounding — courts encourage settlement
4. Apply for bail immediately — offence is bailable
5. Explore Lok Adalat for faster settlement

IMPORTANT NUMBERS TO KNOW
- Section 138: Main offence — cheque dishonour for insufficient funds
- Section 141: Company cheques — directors/officers also liable if they were in charge of business
- Section 142: Cognizance only on complaint by payee/holder
- Section 143A: Interim compensation order by trial court
- Section 148: Mandatory deposit on appeal
