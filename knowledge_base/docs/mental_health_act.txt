MENTAL HEALTHCARE ACT 2017 — RIGHTS OF PERSONS WITH MENTAL ILLNESS

The Mental Healthcare Act 2017 (MHCA 2017) recognises that persons with mental illness have the same rights as any other citizen and ensures their dignity, autonomy, and access to mental health care.

============================
KEY RIGHTS OF PERSONS WITH MENTAL ILLNESS
============================

RIGHT TO ACCESS MENTAL HEALTH CARE (Section 18):
Every person has the right to access mental health care and treatment from mental health services run or funded by the appropriate government. The government is obligated to make mental health care accessible, affordable, and of good quality.

RIGHT TO COMMUNITY LIVING (Section 19):
A person with mental illness has the right to live in the community. They CANNOT be forced to live in a mental health establishment unless they need intensive treatment that cannot be provided in community settings.

RIGHT TO PROTECTION FROM CRUEL, INHUMAN TREATMENT (Section 20):
- No person with mental illness shall be subjected to electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) without anaesthesia and muscle relaxants
- No ECT to children
- Seclusion and restraint only as last resort, for shortest duration, following prescribed procedure
- Any physical, sexual, or psychological abuse of patient in mental health establishment: Criminal offence

RIGHT TO EQUALITY AND NON-DISCRIMINATION (Section 21):
Every person with mental illness has the right to the same civil and political rights as any other citizen: right to vote, right to property, right to marry and have family life, right to education and employment.

RIGHT TO CONFIDENTIALITY (Section 23):
All information about mental illness of a person is strictly confidential. Cannot be shared without patient's consent (except for safety, public health, or legal proceedings).

RIGHT TO ADVANCE DIRECTIVE (Section 5):
A person can make an advance directive specifying:
- How they wish to be cared for if they lose capacity in future
- What treatment they do NOT want
- Who should be their nominated representative
Doctors MUST honour the advance directive unless it endangers the person or others.

RIGHT TO NOMINATED REPRESENTATIVE (Section 14):
Every person with mental illness has the right to appoint a nominated representative (family member, friend, or any other person) to take decisions on their behalf if they cannot.

RIGHT TO FREE LEGAL SERVICES (Section 29):
Every person with mental illness is entitled to free legal services under the Legal Services Authorities Act.

SPECIAL RIGHTS IN MENTAL HEALTH ESTABLISHMENTS:
- Right to be informed about illness, treatment, and medication in understandable language
- Right to refuse treatment (if capable of making decision)
- Right to give or withhold consent for treatment
- Right to receive a second opinion
- Right to receive visitors
- Right to communicate privately
- Right to religious observances

============================
ADMISSION TO MENTAL HEALTH ESTABLISHMENTS
============================

VOLUNTARY ADMISSION: Person with mental illness above 18 years can voluntarily admit themselves for treatment. Can discharge themselves at any time.

SUPPORTED ADMISSION (for minors and incapacitated adults):
Guardian/nominated representative can seek admission. Must have assessment by psychiatrist.

INDEPENDENT ADMISSION (for minors above 12 years with capacity):
Minor can admit themselves. Guardian must be informed.

INVOLUNTARY ADMISSION (Sections 98–103):
Against person's will only if:
1. Person has mental illness and
2. Cannot make valid treatment decisions AND
3. Has a history of violence OR imminent danger to self/others
Two-step process: Magistrate's order after assessment by two psychiatrists.
Period: Maximum 30 days initially, extendable by Magistrate.

MENTAL HEALTH REVIEW BOARD (MHRB):
Every district has a MHRB that reviews all admissions, hears complaints from patients and relatives, and can order discharge.
Contact MHRB if you believe a person is being illegally detained in a mental health establishment.

============================
SUICIDE ATTEMPT — DECRIMINALIZED
============================

SECTION 115 MHCA: Attempt to suicide (Section 309 IPC / BNS Section 226) is presumed to be a result of severe stress. Any person who attempts suicide shall NOT be tried and punished under the law.
The government has a duty to provide care, treatment, and rehabilitation to reduce stress.
Police CANNOT register a criminal case against a person for attempting suicide.

If police or anyone threatens to prosecute for attempted suicide: This is illegal and can be challenged before the Magistrate.

============================
MENTAL ILLNESS AND EMPLOYMENT
============================

Employers CANNOT discriminate against persons with mental illness in:
- Recruitment
- Promotion
- Terms and conditions of employment
Such discrimination is a violation of the MHCA and RPWD Act (Rights of Persons with Disabilities — mental illness is a recognized disability under RPWD 2016).
