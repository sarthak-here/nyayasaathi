LEGAL AID, BAIL, RIGHTS OF ARRESTED PERSONS — BNSS 2023
=========================================================
(Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita replaced CrPC w.e.f. July 1, 2024)

RIGHTS OF ARRESTED PERSON (Sections 35–60 BNSS)

Right to know grounds of arrest (Section 47):
Police MUST inform you of the FULL particulars of the offence for which you are arrested — at the time of arrest.
If arrested for a bailable offence, police MUST inform you of your right to bail.

Right to inform family (Section 48):
Police MUST immediately inform a relative, friend, or nominated person about:
- The fact of your arrest.
- The place where you are held.
This is a MANDATORY duty — failure by police is actionable.

Right to free legal aid:
If you cannot afford a lawyer, you have a fundamental right to free legal representation (Article 22(1) of Constitution + Legal Services Authorities Act 1987).
Request free legal aid from District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) immediately.
Duty Counsel is available at police stations and courts — available 24/7 for arrested persons.
Call NALSA helpline: 15100.

Right against illegal detention (Section 57):
No arrested person can be held in police custody for more than 24 HOURS without a Magistrate's remand order.
This 24-hour rule is absolute — if violated, the detention is illegal (unlawful detention).
If held illegally: File Habeas Corpus petition in High Court immediately.

Right to medical examination (Section 51):
Any arrested person can request medical examination by a government medical officer.
Critical if you have injuries at time of arrest (to document and protect against false custodial violence claims against you, and to document actual custodial violence).

Right against custodial torture:
Custodial torture and third-degree methods are unconstitutional (Article 21).
If tortured in custody: File complaint with NHRC (nhrc.nic.in | 14433), State Human Rights Commission, or petition High Court.
File complaint to Superintendent of Police and District Magistrate.
National Human Rights Commission can recommend compensation and prosecution.

BAIL — BNSS SECTIONS 478–498

BAILABLE OFFENCES — BAIL IS YOUR ABSOLUTE RIGHT (Section 478):
For bailable offences, bail CANNOT be refused by police or courts.
Bail must be given on personal recognizance (your own bond) or surety.
Police officer at police station must release you on bail.

NON-BAILABLE OFFENCES — BAIL IS AT COURT'S DISCRETION (Section 480):
Apply for bail before the Magistrate (for lesser offences) or Sessions Court (for serious offences).
Court considers: Gravity of offence, prior record, flight risk, witness tampering risk.

DEFAULT BAIL (Section 480) — VERY IMPORTANT:
If police fail to file chargesheet within:
90 days: For offences punishable with death, life imprisonment, or 10+ years.
60 days: For all other offences.
You are ENTITLED TO BAIL as of right. Apply to Magistrate for default bail.
This is also called "statutory bail" — court CANNOT deny it if conditions are met.

DEFAULT BAIL FOR FIRST-TIME OFFENDERS (Section 479 — NEW IN BNSS):
If you have NEVER been convicted previously, and you have served:
1/3rd of the maximum sentence for your offence while as undertrial —
You are entitled to bail as of right.
Example: Offence with maximum 3-year sentence — after 1 year in jail as undertrial, entitled to bail.
The Jail Superintendent has a duty to proactively bring such cases to court.
Exception: Offences punishable with death or life imprisonment.

ANTICIPATORY BAIL (Section 482):
If you have reason to believe you may be arrested for a non-bailable offence:
Apply to Sessions Court or High Court for anticipatory bail BEFORE arrest.
Court can grant pre-arrest bail with conditions.
SC/ST Atrocities Act cases: Anticipatory bail is generally not available (Supreme Court ruling).

FIR RIGHTS

Zero FIR (Section 173 BNSS):
You can file an FIR at ANY police station anywhere in India, regardless of where the incident occurred.
Police MUST register the FIR, then transfer it to the jurisdictional police station.
No police station can refuse a Zero FIR.

If police refuse to register FIR:
Step 1: Get your written complaint dated and stamped as "received" by the duty officer.
Step 2: Send complaint by registered post to Superintendent of Police (SP).
Step 3: File complaint under BNSS Section 175 before Judicial Magistrate — Magistrate can direct FIR registration.
Step 4: High Court writ petition (Mandamus) if all else fails.

FREE LEGAL AID — WHO IS ENTITLED?

Under Legal Services Authorities Act 1987, the following get FREE legal representation:
- Persons with annual income below state-specified limit (usually Rs. 1 lakh to Rs. 3 lakh).
- Women (for all matters relating to women).
- Children.
- Persons in custody.
- Persons with disability.
- Victims of trafficking, disasters, violence.
- SC/ST persons.
- Industrial workmen.
- Mass disaster victims.

HOW TO GET FREE LEGAL AID:
Visit or call your District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) — present in every district.
National helpline: 15100.
Tell them you cannot afford a lawyer — they will assign a lawyer at no cost.
Retainer lawyers are available on duty at all courts.

RIGHTS DURING TRIAL
- Right to speedy trial (not held in custody for years without trial completion).
- Right to cross-examine prosecution witnesses.
- Right to present your defense.
- Right to be informed of charges in a language you understand.
- Right to an interpreter if you don't understand the proceedings language.

POLICE ENCOUNTERS
If a police encounter (alleged) happens to a family member:
File complaint with NHRC (nhrc.nic.in) immediately.
State Human Rights Commission.
File FIR against police officers involved (BNS Section 103/105 as applicable).
Petition High Court for independent inquiry.
