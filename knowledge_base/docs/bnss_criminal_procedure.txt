BHARATIYA NAGARIK SURAKSHA SANHITA (BNSS) 2023 — CRIMINAL PROCEDURE
(Replaces Code of Criminal Procedure 1973 from July 1, 2024)

The BNSS governs the procedure for investigation, trial, and punishment of criminal offences in India.

ARREST — YOUR RIGHTS (Sections 47–60 BNSS)

Rights on Arrest:
1. Right to be informed of grounds of arrest immediately (Section 47)
2. Right to inform a friend, relative, or nominated person of your arrest (Section 48) — police must inform within 12 hours
3. Right to consult a lawyer of your choice at any stage (Section 50)
4. Right to be produced before a Magistrate within 24 hours (Section 57)
5. Right against handcuffing unless person is habitual criminal, previously escaped, or poses danger — must be justified in writing (Section 43)
6. Right to be examined by a medical officer after arrest if requested (Section 51)
7. Woman can only be arrested by female police officer. Between 6 PM and 6 AM, arrest requires special permission of Magistrate.

Arrested person's details must be displayed in the police station.

BAIL (Sections 478–501 BNSS)

Regular Bail: Granted by Magistrate or Sessions Court after arrest.

Anticipatory Bail (Section 482): Applied for BEFORE arrest when there is reasonable apprehension of arrest. If granted, police cannot arrest you.
- Apply in Sessions Court or High Court
- Court can impose conditions (report to police, surrender passport, etc.)
- Factors considered: nature of offence, antecedents, possibility of fleeing

Default Bail (Section 479): If police fail to file chargesheet within:
- 60 days: for offences punishable up to 10 years
- 90 days: for offences punishable with death/life/10+ years
You are entitled to bail as a matter of right. File application immediately.

Bail for Bailable Offences: Police or Magistrate MUST grant bail. You cannot be refused.

Bail Conditions: Magistrate may impose conditions — attending police station, not leaving city, surrendering passport.

SUMMONS AND WARRANTS (Sections 63–89 BNSS)
Summons: Issued to compel attendance in court. Must be served personally.
Warrant: Can be bailable (bail can be taken) or non-bailable.
Search Warrant: Police must obtain from Magistrate before searching a premises (except in emergency/hot pursuit).

FIR — FIRST INFORMATION REPORT (Section 173 BNSS)
- Any person can file an FIR at ANY police station (Zero FIR) regardless of where the offence occurred
- Police CANNOT refuse to register an FIR for a cognizable offence
- After registering FIR, police must give free copy to complainant immediately
- FIR is now mandatory in electronic form
- If police refuse: write to SP (Superintendent of Police)
- If still refused: file complaint before Magistrate under Section 175(3)
- Online FIR: Can be filed on state police portals for non-serious offences

INVESTIGATION (Sections 173–199 BNSS)
- Police have 60 or 90 days to file chargesheet (challan) depending on offence severity
- Victim has right to be informed of progress
- Statement of victims (especially women/children) must be recorded at their residence by female officer
- Audio-video recording of statement of victim of rape/acid attack is mandatory

TRIAL PROCEDURE
Magistrate's Trial: For offences punishable up to 3 years (summary trial) or up to 7 years
Sessions Trial: For serious offences (rape, murder, robbery, etc.)

Plea Bargaining (Sections 289–301 BNSS):
- Available for offences punishable up to 7 years (not against women/children)
- Accused pleads guilty, sentence is reduced (minimum 1/4th of minimum punishment)
- Saves time and resources

Trial Timeline (New in BNSS):
- Framing of charges: Within 60 days of first hearing
- Evidence must conclude within 60 days after charges framed
- Judgment within 45 days of completion of arguments
- Total target: serious cases to be disposed in 3 years

RIGHTS OF ACCUSED DURING TRIAL
1. Right to know charges framed against them
2. Right to cross-examine prosecution witnesses
3. Right to present defence witnesses
4. Right to free legal aid if cannot afford lawyer
5. Right to get copy of FIR, chargesheet, and all documents free of cost
6. Right to be present at every hearing
7. Right to appeal against conviction

RIGHTS OF VICTIM AND COMPLAINANT
1. Right to be informed of progress of investigation
2. Right to a copy of FIR free of cost
3. Right to be heard before bail is granted to accused (Section 479)
4. Right to appeal if magistrate refuses to take cognizance
5. Right to compensation (Section 396 BNSS) — court can order compensation to victim
6. Witness protection scheme for vulnerable witnesses

TRIAL IN ABSENTIA (New): Accused absconding for 90+ days can be tried and convicted without their presence.

ELECTRONIC EVIDENCE (New in BNSS)
Electronic records, including WhatsApp messages, emails, CCTV footage, and call records are admissible as evidence with proper certification.

SUMMONS BY ELECTRONIC MEANS (Section 63): Courts can serve summons via email, WhatsApp, or other electronic means.

APPEALS
- Against Magistrate's order: Sessions Court
- Against Sessions Court: High Court
- Against High Court: Supreme Court (Article 136 — Special Leave Petition)
- Time limit: 30 days for appeal against conviction (extensions possible)

POLICE ACCOUNTABILITY
If police torture or use excessive force:
- File complaint with Superintendent of Police
- File complaint with State Human Rights Commission
- File complaint with National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) — nhrc.nic.in
- File writ petition in High Court
- File complaint under Section 132 BNS (voluntarily causing hurt by public servant)
