INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY RIGHTS IN INDIA
(Trade Marks Act 1999, Copyright Act 1957, Patents Act 1970, Designs Act 2000, Geographical Indications Act 1999)

============================
TRADE MARKS ACT 1999
============================

A trademark is a sign (word, logo, shape, sound, colour) that distinguishes goods/services of one person from those of others.

REGISTRATION:
Apply online at ipindia.gov.in (Intellectual Property India). Processing: 12–18 months typically.
Classes: 45 classes (Nice Classification) — file in class(es) relevant to your goods/services.
Duration: 10 years, renewable indefinitely every 10 years.

RIGHTS OF TRADEMARK OWNER:
- Exclusive right to use the mark for registered goods/services
- Right to sue for infringement (use of identical/similar mark for same/similar goods)
- Right to seek injunction (stop the infringer), damages, and account of profits
- Right to use ® symbol (only after registration)
- TM symbol can be used even before registration (on pending application)

INFRINGEMENT (Section 29):
Using a mark identical or deceptively similar to a registered trademark without permission.
Penalty (Criminal): Imprisonment 6 months to 3 years; fine Rs. 50,000 to Rs. 2 lakh.
Civil remedy: Injunction, damages, delivery-up of infringing goods.

PASSING OFF:
Even unregistered marks can be protected under the common law tort of passing off — if you have goodwill and reputation, and another person falsely represents their goods as yours.

WELL-KNOWN MARKS: Marks like Tata, Amul, Reliance are declared "well-known" — protected across all classes. No one can register or use a deceptively similar mark even for unrelated goods.

DOMAIN NAME DISPUTES:
WIPO Arbitration Center and IN Registry (.in domain) handle cybersquatting disputes.

============================
COPYRIGHT ACT 1957 (Amended 2012)
============================

Copyright protects original literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works; cinematographic films; and sound recordings.

AUTOMATIC PROTECTION: Copyright arises the moment an original work is created. NO REGISTRATION REQUIRED. However, registration (at Copyright Office, New Delhi) creates presumptive proof of ownership.

DURATION:
- Literary, dramatic, musical, artistic works: Author's lifetime + 60 years
- Cinematographic films and sound recordings: 60 years from publication
- Government works: 60 years from publication

RIGHTS OF COPYRIGHT OWNER:
- Reproduce the work (print, photocopy, digital copies)
- Issue copies to the public (sale, rental)
- Perform/broadcast the work
- Translate or adapt the work
- Make cinematographic film

INFRINGEMENT:
Copying, reproducing, distributing, or performing a copyrighted work without licence.
Civil remedy: Injunction + damages + accounts of profits
Criminal: Imprisonment up to 3 years; fine Rs. 50,000 to Rs. 2 lakh.

FAIR USE / FAIR DEALING (Section 52):
Not infringement to use work for:
- Private or personal use (research, study)
- Criticism or review (with acknowledgment)
- Reporting current events (newspapers, TV)
- Judicial proceedings
- Educational purposes (classroom teaching)

MORAL RIGHTS (Section 57):
Author retains the right to claim authorship and object to any distortion/mutilation of work that would harm their reputation — EVEN AFTER SELLING COPYRIGHT.

MUSIC ROYALTIES:
Radio stations, streaming platforms, event organizers must pay royalties to copyright owners. Copyright Societies (IPRS for music, PPL for sound recordings) collect and distribute royalties.
If platform/event uses your music without permission: Send legal notice + file complaint with Copyright Office.

============================
PATENTS ACT 1970 (Amended 2005)
============================

A patent grants the inventor exclusive rights to make, use, and sell the invention for 20 years.

WHAT IS PATENTABLE:
New invention + Inventive step (non-obvious) + Industrial application.

WHAT CANNOT BE PATENTED:
- Discoveries of natural phenomena or abstract theories
- Mathematical methods, business methods, mental acts
- Computer programs per se (software patents are controversial in India)
- Mere discovery of a new form of a known substance (without enhanced efficacy — Section 3(d) blocks ever-greening of drug patents)
- Agricultural/horticultural methods
- Medical treatment methods

COMPULSORY LICENCE (Section 84):
After 3 years of patent grant, any person can apply for compulsory licence if:
- Reasonable requirements of public are not being met
- Patented invention is not available at reasonably affordable price
- Patented invention is not worked in India
Notable example: Natco Pharma v. Bayer Corporation (2012) — compulsory licence for cancer drug Nexavar.

GEOGRAPHIC INDICATIONS (GI):
Products with specific geographical origin and qualities attributable to that origin.
Examples: Darjeeling Tea, Basmati Rice, Chanderi Saree, Kanchipuram Silk, Alphonso Mango.
GI tags are registered with GI Registry (Chennai). Producer groups can register. Prevents misuse by outsiders.

============================
DESIGNS ACT 2000
============================

Protects the visual appearance (shape, configuration, pattern, ornament, or composition of lines or colours) of an article.
Registration: 10 years + 5 year renewal = 15 years maximum.
Industrial designs (furniture, textiles, electronics appearance) protected. Pure art is copyright.

WHAT TO DO IF YOUR IP IS STOLEN:
1. Document ownership — timestamps, records, registered certificates
2. Send cease and desist letter to infringer
3. File complaint with police for criminal action
4. File civil suit for injunction and damages in District Court / High Court
5. For online infringement: File DMCA/notice with platform (YouTube, Amazon, etc.)
6. Customs: Record trademark with Customs authorities — they can seize infringing imports
