LANDLORD-TENANT DISPUTES, ILLEGAL EVICTION, RENT LAWS
=======================================================

OVERVIEW
Landlord-tenant relationships are primarily governed by state Rent Control Acts. Most states have their own rent laws. Key principles are universal — landlords CANNOT forcibly evict, cannot harass tenants, and cannot enter premises without notice.

TENANT'S FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS

No forcible eviction without court order:
A landlord CANNOT physically throw out a tenant, change locks, cut electricity/water, or remove belongings to force eviction — regardless of any dispute.
Doing so = criminal trespass (BNS Section 329/331) + wrongful restraint + criminal intimidation (BNS 351).
If landlord does this: File FIR at police station immediately.

Eviction can ONLY happen through:
1. Rent Controller Court/Civil Court order.
2. Mutual agreement and voluntary vacation.

GROUNDS FOR LEGAL EVICTION (under most Rent Control Acts):
1. Non-payment of rent for 2+ months (after written notice).
2. Subletting without permission.
3. Using premises for illegal purpose.
4. Causing damage to the property.
5. Landlord needing property for bona fide personal use (with notice and compensation in most states).
6. Demolition/reconstruction (with notice).

RENT INCREASES:
Landlords cannot arbitrarily increase rent. Most Rent Control Acts limit annual rent increase to 5-10% or link to government formula.
Rent agreement specifying higher increase may be challenged before Rent Controller.

SECURITY DEPOSIT:
Security deposit is refundable — landlord must return it within 30-45 days of tenant vacating (varies by state).
Landlord can only deduct for actual damage beyond normal wear and tear — must provide itemized list.
If deposit not returned without reason: Civil suit for recovery + Consumer Forum (if residential).

WHAT TO DO IF LANDLORD IS HARASSING YOU

If landlord cuts power/water:
File FIR for criminal breach under BNS Section 329 + Section 351 (criminal intimidation).
Get emergency injunction from Civil Court to restore utilities.
Electricity board complaint for unauthorized disconnection.

If landlord changes locks or removes belongings:
File FIR for house-trespass (BNS 331) and theft (BNS 303).
Seek urgent/emergency relief from Civil Court for restoration.

If landlord enters without notice/consent:
File complaint for criminal trespass (BNS 329).
Seek injunction from Civil Court.

If landlord threatens you:
File FIR for criminal intimidation (BNS 351).

HOW TO FILE FOR LEGAL EVICTION — FOR LANDLORDS

Step 1: Send written notice to tenant specifying the reason for eviction and giving 30-60 days (varies by state and reason).
Step 2: If tenant doesn't vacate, file eviction petition before Rent Controller/appropriate court.
Step 3: Tenant has right to contest. Court hears both sides.
Step 4: If eviction decree passed and not obeyed, get execution warrant.

RENT AGREEMENTS AND REGISTRATION

Rent agreements of 11 months: Most common in India. 11-month agreements avoid mandatory registration. Renewable annually.
Agreements of 12+ months: Must be registered with Sub-Registrar under Registration Act 1908. Stamp duty applicable.
Unregistered 12+ month agreements cannot be used as evidence in court.
License vs. Lease: Most urban residential agreements are "leave and license" — this gives tenant fewer protections but landlord can recover possession more easily after notice period.

PAYING GUEST (PG) ACCOMMODATION:
PG residents have basic consumer rights — right to safety, basic amenities as promised.
Withholding PG fees, harassment, arbitrary eviction can be challenged at Consumer Forum.

COMMERCIAL TENANTS:
Commercial tenants have fewer protections than residential tenants in most states.
Transfer of Property Act 1882 governs in absence of state rent control law.
Commercial tenants still protected against forcible eviction — must go through court.

SOCIETY/APARTMENT-LEVEL DISPUTES:
Maintenance fee disputes: Approach cooperative housing society Registrar (state government).
Society cannot cut water/lift access for non-payment without proper notice and legal process.
Bye-law violations: Complaint to Registrar of Cooperative Societies.

RELEVANT STATE RENT LAWS (key ones):
Maharashtra: Maharashtra Rent Control Act 1999.
Delhi: Delhi Rent Act 1958 / Delhi Rent Control Act.
Karnataka: Karnataka Rent Act 2001.
Tamil Nadu: Tamil Nadu Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act 1960.
Gujarat: Gujarat Rent Control Act 1999.
West Bengal: West Bengal Premises Tenancy Act 1997.
Note: Contact local DLSA for your state's specific provisions.

DOCUMENTS TENANT SHOULD PRESERVE:
Rent agreement / leave and license agreement.
Rent receipts (get receipt for every payment in cash — insist on this).
Bank transfer records if paying by bank.
Any written communication with landlord.
Photographs of property condition at entry (to counter damage claims at exit).

==========================================================================
IF YOU ARE THE LANDLORD — DEFENSE RIGHTS IN TENANT DISPUTES
==========================================================================

YOUR RIGHTS AS LANDLORD:
- Right to evict a tenant through court for non-payment, subletting, illegal use, or bona fide personal need — but ONLY through proper legal process.
- Right to claim unpaid rent as arrears in Rent Controller Court.
- Right to damages for any property destroyed or damaged by tenant.

WHAT TENANT MUST PROVE TO RESIST EVICTION:
- That they have been paying rent (rent receipts are critical).
- That the eviction ground claimed by landlord does not exist or is false.
- For "bona fide personal need" evictions: Tenant can challenge whether the landlord genuinely needs the property or is using it as pretext.

COMMON DEFENSES FOR LANDLORDS AGAINST FALSE TENANT CLAIMS:
Forcible eviction allegation: If tenant claims you forcibly evicted them but you have a court order, produce it. If tenant left voluntarily and is now claiming forced eviction, produce evidence of voluntary vacation.
Non-payment of rent: Produce rent ledger, demand notices, bank records showing rent was not received.
Tenant misusing property for illegal purposes: Produce evidence (police complaint, witness statements) of the misuse.

IF TENANT FILES FALSE POLICE COMPLAINT AGAINST YOU:
Collect evidence showing you did not commit criminal trespass — if you entered your own property with legal authority, document it.
Apply for anticipatory bail if arrest is threatened.
Challenge the FIR in High Court if it is based on false facts.

WHEN TENANT CAN BE SUED CRIMINALLY:
If tenant damages the property wilfully: BNS Section 324 (mischief).
If tenant refuses to vacate after court order: Contempt of court.
If tenant threatens or assaults landlord: BNS Section 351/115.
